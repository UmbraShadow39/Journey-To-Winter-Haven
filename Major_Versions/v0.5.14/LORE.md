# Journey to Winter Haven — World Lore Bible

> This document captures the foundational lore, cosmology, and worldbuilding for the
> Journey to Winter Haven trilogy and its expanded universe. Update this as new ideas develop.

---

## The World

### Locations — Game 1
- **Winter Haven** — primary city, home of the arena, controlled by the Divine faction. Situated at the **base of Forstback Mountain** — a gateway town that controls access to everything above it. Whoever controls Winter Haven controls who gets strong enough to enter the mountains. This is a core reason the Beast Gods chose it for the arena.
- **Sky Harbor** — second major city, Beast God influence, gem enhancement hub
- **Frostveil Peak / Forstback Mountain** — mountain range rising directly above Winter Haven. The upper reaches are where the endgame gear questline takes place. Legendary armor pieces and the Dwarven city questline are found here. Dangerous enough that only players who have cleared the arena have any business attempting it.
- **Ashenveil** — city at the edge of the wilderness, home of the Warrior class origin. Named for the ash and mist that rolls in from the forest. Feels lived-in and battle-worn. Gateway to the wilds beyond. Home of the Ashen Vanguard guild.
- **Ashen Frost Forest** — the forest between Ashenveil and Winter Haven. Named for its ash-colored trees and the frost that creeps in from the mountain as you travel deeper. Misty, eerie, and atmospheric. Creatures live here but the path through is a known travel route — dangerous enough to respect, manageable enough that a trained Vanguard member can pass through. The frost in the name is the first subtle hint of Winter Haven before the player ever arrives. Not a playable dungeon — narrative flavor and travel atmosphere only.
- **[Wizard Town — TBD]** — a smaller magical settlement, not on par with Sky Harbor but with its own arcane significance. Likely built around something old and not fully understood. Origin city for the Mage class.

### The Arena
The arena in Winter Haven is the central stage of Game 1. On the surface it appears to be
a combat proving ground, but it is actually a harvesting mechanism — both the Divine faction
and the Beast Gods feed on human emotional energy generated through combat and suffering.
Neither side is purely good or evil. Both exploit humanity, just with different philosophies.

### The Coming of Age Tradition
- The best young hominids attempt the Winter Haven dungeon around age 16 as a **rite of passage**
- Most struggle. Some die. The dungeon is calibrated to be survivable for the exceptional — and lethal for the average
- The protagonist is notably more gifted than peers from the first room, advancing further than anyone expects
- Neither the protagonist nor anyone around them fully understands why — the answer is divine blood
- The tradition is culturally celebrated but the dungeon's true purpose as an emotional farm is not publicly known
- The Beast Gods benefit doubly: coming of age fear and desperate hope is among the richest emotional harvests possible

### Winter Haven — The Dungeon (Expanded Lore)
- The dungeon beneath Winter Haven is not a natural cave system — it is an **engineered emotional farm**
- Treasure and danger are calibrated bait designed to generate peak emotional output — hope, fear, desperation
- The Beast Gods harvest the most potent emotions, particularly the moment a player realises they won't make it out
- Medicine is available inside the dungeon — **not enough to save you, just enough to reduce pain and prolong suffering**
- This is intentional: prolonged fear and suffering yields a richer emotional harvest than a quick death
- The dungeon is guarded by **Beast farm hands** at its deepest levels

## Faction Ideologies

### The Divine Faction
Controls **Sky Harbor tower**. Represents order, structure, and protection — but ultimately
still harvests human emotional energy for power. Their methods are more palatable: they feed
on **worship, joy, and happiness** rather than suffering. The tower is a place of learning
and structure, genuinely beneficial to those within it — but the emotional harvest beneath
that benevolence is real. The Divine faction is not evil, but it is not free of sin either.

### The Beast Gods
Control **Winter Haven dungeon**. Represent survival through strength, combat, and raw power.
Believe the strong shape the world and that humans and monsters exist in eternal competition.
Brutal and honest about what they are — they do not dress up exploitation as benevolence.

The dungeon produces loot and prosperity to keep the population willing and emotionally rich.
The Beast Gods harvest the most potent emotions — fear, desperation, grief. The farmhands on
the deep floors maintain the harvest cycle.

> The ideological conflict between these two factions is the true engine of the story.
> The Fallen Warrior is not the cause — he is merely the flashpoint that forces the player
> to choose a side.

## The Moral Choice System

The game features a four-ending moral framework:
- **Pure Good**
- **Good Neutral**
- **Evil Neutral**
- **Pure Evil**

Moral choices carry forward into subsequent games via save state. Every decision the player
makes — including how they handle the Fallen Warrior's essence — shapes which ending they
receive and which path opens in the sequel.

### Dungeon Path Escalation

Floor 3 is the **last floor accessible to both paths** before moral alignment determines progression. The divergence is not a hard wall — it is a gradual escalation that mirrors how corruption and redemption actually work. Nobody becomes purely evil overnight.

**Winter Haven (Good Path escalation)**
- Floors 1–3: Moral neutrality — spare the misled, measured choices, not everything is black and white
- Floor 4+: Harder calls — genuine evil starts appearing, mercy has real costs
- Mid floors: Skilled hominid fighters — arena veterans who went too deep and never came back
- Deep floors: Something starts feeling wrong. Enemies that don't fit natural evolution.
  Hominids that have been changed. Monster laboratory hidden beneath the harvesting operation.
- Boss floors: Beast God experiments that worked. The ones they kept. Each drops a legendary armor piece.
- Endgame: The Divine farmhands on the deepest floor guard the truth of what the dungeon really is.
  The player must decide: banish the farmhands and break the harvest cycle (losing the dungeon's
  gifts), or ascend and become a recognised demi-god and new Guardian of Winter Haven — keeping
  the cycle intact.
- The reveal: Medicine was never enough to save you — just enough to prolong the harvest.
  The Divine side is real and the protection is real. So is the farm.

**Sky Harbor (Evil Path escalation)**
- Floors 1–3: Blend in through learning — a seemingly pure tower, teachers extend grace
- Floor 4+: Bribing, manipulation, cutting corners — the institution begins to notice
- Deep floors: The tower's corruption becomes visible. Intimidation, betrayal, deliberate harm.
  Beast God influence has seeped down further than anyone admits.
- Endgame: The tower guardian — a low-tier Divine being — stands at the top between the player
  and full corruption. Two choices: kill the guardian and purify the tower (world stays neutral),
  or let the tower guardian become your master (fully corrupts the tower, world shifts to Evil 1).
- The tragedy: The tower wanted to be something better. You helped decide whether it gets to be.

### The Four Endings (Detailed)

**Pure Evil**
- Fully corrupt Sky Harbor — let the tower guardian become your master
- Kill the Divine farmhands in Winter Haven — end their harvest, seize the dungeon's power for yourself
- Both pillars of the world are corrupted or destroyed. You have immense power and a ruined world to rule.
- The Beast Gods' favour is total. The scars of what you did follow into Game 2.

**Evil Neutral**
- Let the tower guardian become your master (Sky Harbor fully corrupted)
- Leave Winter Haven's Divine harvest intact — the farm continues, people benefit, you take your cut
- The most comfortable evil ending. You're complicit in two systems of exploitation.
- The most human choice on the evil side — maximum personal gain, minimum personal cost.

**Good Neutral**
- Banish the Sky Harbor corruption — spare the tower guardian, keep the world balanced
- Leave Winter Haven's Divine harvest intact — you couldn't bear to take that from the people
- Your past actions have permanent costs. Some things cannot be undone.
- You did right at the end but you cannot undo what the journey cost. Haunted.

**Pure Good**
- Drive out the Divine farmhands in Winter Haven — break the harvest cycle entirely
- Purify Sky Harbor — kill or banish the Beast God corruption, spare the guardian
- Freedom has a cost: Winter Haven's prosperity may collapse without the farm sustaining it
- People face an uncertain future. You chose truth and liberation over comfortable exploitation.
- The hardest ending to achieve. The one with the most uncertain aftermath. The most honest.

### The Fallen Warrior Choice
A pivotal early moral decision:
- **Crush his essence (Good)** — sets him free. The arena erupts, sand drains, Young Chimera erupts from below. A mysterious figure freezes time, heals the player fully (+2 temp max AP, all status cleared), whispers *"We will meet again."* → Chimera fight.
  Reward: Chimera Scale on victory. Story flag: `crushed_essence`.
- **Return his essence (Evil)** — hand it to the Beast Gods. Gold drops immediately. The Weapon Core is corrupted into the Tainted Blade (+2 ATK, -3 DEF). Patronus drops into the arena, the Beast Gods' shield crackles around the player (full heal, +2 temp max AP), cracks when Patronus hits it, *"You die NOW."* → Patronus fight.
  Reward: Tainted Champion's Breastplate on victory (+7 DEF, -5 HP). Story flag: `returned_essence`.

### Young Chimera — Intervention System
- Player must survive **4 full combat cycles** (player turn + enemy turn = 1 cycle) to prove their worth
- **Die cycles 1–3** → regular game over, no intervention
- **Die cycle 4+** → the mysterious figure freezes time again and stabilises the player (1/3 HP restore, status clear), *"Go now. I am limited in how much I can intervene."*
  - Story flag: `chimera_alive` — the Chimera is still out there
  - The son hunts it down 30 years later as a mission of revenge
- **Win** → Chimera is vanquished, Chimera Scale drops
  - Story flag: `chimera_vanquished`

### Patronus — Intervention System (Evil Path)
- Same cycle-based structure as the Chimera fight
- Player must survive **4 full combat cycles** before intervention is possible
- **Die cycles 1–3** → regular game over
- **Die cycle 4+** → Beast Gods intervene — an unbreakable shield surrounds the player, Patronus teleported out in rage. *"He will have his revenge."*
- **Win** → Patronus survives (demi-god, cannot be killed outright) but drops his legendary shield
  - Guardian of Winter Haven later appears with a weaker replacement shield
  - Story flag: `patronus_shield_dropped`

## Key Characters

### Umbra (The Warrior / The Father)
- The warrior protagonist of the **prologue/tutorial** — this is the father's story, set 30 years before Game 1
- **Forced into the tournament** — not a willing gladiator seeking glory, an ordinary man thrust into extraordinary circumstances
- The tutorial framing is intentional: the player learns all core mechanics through genuine high stakes, not a contrived training sequence
- Player experience and character experience are synchronized — both figuring it out as they go
- Has **diluted divine blood** — enough to briefly break the Fallen's control at the moral choice moment
- The latent divine heritage is the in-lore reason the moral choice is available at all
- His alignment choices shape what world his son inherits 30 years later — weight the player doesn't fully grasp on a first playthrough
- Cannot safely raise his child due to enemies made on either path — entrusts the son to Herb and Gloria

### Prologue Notes — The Road to Winter Haven
- Umbra is **19 years old**, fresh out of Vanguard training
- His first quest: travel to Winter Haven, **confirm the dungeon exists**, clear the first floor, report back to the Ashen Vanguard
- Aldric quietly arranged this mission for Umbra — he thought it was safe, an easy win for a greenhorn
- Rumors of a **beast tournament** in Winter Haven exist in Ashenveil but are dismissed as tavern talk — no hard evidence, no reliable witnesses
- The dungeon rumors are old and unconfirmed — nobody senior thought it was worth their time
- Umbra travels through **Ashen Frost Forest** — hints of danger but no major encounters, narrative flavor only
- He arrives at Winter Haven at night, tired from the road
- **Bo intercepts him before he reaches the dungeon entrance**
- Umbra never completes his official mission — but survives something far worse

### Aldric (Umbra's Father)
- Senior member of the **Ashen Vanguard**, based in Ashenveil
- **A-rank adventurer** — elite, seasoned, one of the most powerful warriors in the guild
- Still has significant room to grow — Triple SSS is the theoretical ceiling, A-rank is far from it
- A genuinely hard-earned rank achieved through decades of combat and survival
- Has a trace of **divine blood** several generations back — enough to give him a slightly higher ceiling than a normal hominid, though he has no knowledge of this
- His reputation in Ashenveil is significant — Umbra grew up in his shadow, which is both motivating and a quiet pressure
- Pronounciation: **AL-drik**

### Elwin (Umbra's Mother)
- A **renowned herbalist** in Ashenveil — her knowledge of plants, remedies, and natural medicine is exceptional
- Her skill is so far beyond normal that people quietly marvel at it — explained away as natural talent and decades of practice
- In reality her herbalist gift is her **divine nature bleeding through** — she is multiple generations removed from a Divine being, the heritage expressing itself as an almost supernatural affinity for healing and natural medicine
- Has no knowledge of her divine bloodline — she is simply very, very good at what she does
- Umbra's efficiency with **First Aid** traces directly to her — he grew up learning her methods
- The combination of Aldric's warrior bloodline and Elwin's healer bloodline looks like exceptional genetics to everyone in Ashenveil. Nobody connects it to anything divine.
- Pronounciation: **EL-win**

### Frostpine Tonic
- A potion crafted personally by Elwin and pressed into Umbra's hand as he leaves Ashenveil for his first quest
- Named for the pine and frost scent of Ashen Frost Forest — it smells like home
- **Effect: restores 40% max HP, clears all status effects, and restores 2 AP**
- Reflects both parents in one item — the heal and status clear is Elwin's master herbalist craft, the AP restore is Aldric's warrior energy
- **Unique item** — cannot be bought, crafted, or found in Game 1. One use only.
- May become obtainable in **New Game Plus** if that mode is ever developed
- Flavor text on use: *"You uncork the small flask. The scent of pine and frost hits you — it smells like home. You feel your wounds close and your strength return."*
- Elwin's sendoff: *"Elwin presses a small flask into your hand at the gate of Ashenveil. 'I made it myself,' she says quietly. 'Your father never took one when he left for his first quest. You're smarter than he was.' She doesn't say goodbye — she just turns and walks back toward the house. She never was good at goodbyes."*
- Mechanically implemented as its own potion type — locked from shops and loot tables

### Divine Lineage — The Hidden Thread
- Umbra is **multiple generations removed** from divine blood on both sides
- Aldric's line carries a warrior's edge — slightly higher physical ceiling than a normal hominid
- Elwin's line carries a healer's gift — affinity for natural medicine and remedies
- Neither parent knows the truth. Neither bloodline is pure enough to trigger obvious divine ability.
- In Umbra the two diluted lines **combine and concentrate** — enough to manifest at the moral choice moment
- In the son (Game 1 protagonist) the concentration is higher still — three generations of convergence, plus the son's mother is an actual Divine being
- The arc from Aldric to Umbra to the son is a quiet generational build toward something neither the Beast Gods nor the Divine faction fully anticipated

### The Fallen Warrior
Arena boss and moral choice trigger. A warrior whose essence is bound to the arena.
His fate is the first major ideological statement the player makes.

### The Son (Official Game 1 Protagonist)
- The **true protagonist of Game 1** — approximately 16 years old, coming of age
- Raised by **Herb and Gloria**, his guardian adoptive parents
- The warrior (Umbra) is his father — the prologue/tutorial is the father's story 30 years in the past
- Attempts the Winter Haven dungeon as part of a **hominid coming of age tradition** — the best children attempt it
- Is notably more gifted than peers without knowing why — **divine blood**, even diluted, sets him apart
- Receives the Weapon Core — either blessed or cursed depending on the father's moral choices 30 years prior
- The nature of the handoff is determined by which faction the father aligned with

### Patronus
- The **last hero to clear all ten floors** of the Winter Haven dungeon
- A **blessed child with divine blood** — demi-god status, granted as part of his deal with the dungeon upon completion
- Appears as an **elderly man** but has looked exactly the same for as long as any villager can remember — his demi-god status and the dungeon's deal have kept him unchanged across centuries
- **Morally grey** — not evil, not good. Made the choice to leave the Beast God harvesting system intact rather than dismantle it. Chose stability over liberation
- Over the last century he has **grown to deeply regret that decision** and now contributes as best he can against the Beast Gods from within the constraints of his deal
- Has **sent many heroes to their deaths** hoping one would finally break the cycle where he could not bring himself to — nobody has succeeded until now
- Acts as a **trainer for the next generation** as they attempt the dungeon — his reward and his punishment simultaneously
- **Protector of Winter Haven** — this was the title and role granted to him by the dungeon upon completion
- Carries a **legendary nano-tech shield** found in the deep treasure caves of the Winter Haven dungeon — powerful artifact, the source of much of his defensive capability
- **Knew the Fallen Warrior** before he was captured — a personal connection that makes the evil path confrontation carry extra weight
- Jumps into the arena and attacks the player if they **choose to make the Fallen Warrior suffer** (evil path trigger)
- Has **Death Defier** — if defeated he does not die but revives at high HP due to demi-god status. The Beast Gods then cut him loose and banish him, stripping his legendary shield
- **Evil path outcome**: Player beats Patronus → he walks out in disgrace, drops a piece of his armour → Tainted Champion's Breastplate drops (+7 DEF, -5 max HP, story flag: `patronus_breastplate_dropped`)
- **Evil path intervention**: If player dies in cycle 4+ the Beast Gods intervene on Patronus's behalf — player escapes, no drop, child later seeks out the shield
- **Skill loadout**: Double Strike (Rank 5) or War Cry (Rank 5) — one of the two, plus Power Charge unlocked as his hidden combo skill (the mark of a true master who has had a century to discover it)

### The Mother (Expanded Lore)
- A **Divine being** — one of the guardians of Sky Harbor
- Cannot openly raise her child without risking discovery by hostile factions
- Watches over the player character from a distance, identity hidden
- If the mage starts in Sky Harbor (good path), they may interact with her unknowingly

### The Father (Expanded Lore)
- Has **enemies regardless of the moral path he took** — good choices and bad choices both create dangerous enemies
- Cannot raise the child safely while being pursued
- May be discoverable as an **optional late-game quest** once the player is powerful enough to handle what finding him brings
- Reunion tone varies dramatically depending on path alignment of both player and father

### Guardian NPCs (All Three Classes)
- Every playable class has a **guardian figure** — an ordinary hominid who raised them in place of absent parents
- No divine blood, no special power, just devotion
- Their mortality and vulnerability grounds each character in human stakes within a world of cosmic power
- The guardian's wellbeing is a potential early emotional driver (e.g. thief stealing medicine for an aging guardian)

## Playable Classes & Origins

### Class Selection
- Class is determined by **weapon choice at the start of the game**, not a character select screen
- Weapon chosen → class assigned → starting location determined
- Feels immersive and lore-consistent: the player becomes something rather than selecting it

### The Warrior
- Starts in Winter Haven / the arena
- Origin already established in Game 1

### The Mage
- Origin: a **controlled dueling tournament** between magic users — more cerebral than the arena
- Moral choice: opponent is vulnerable and outmatched — finish them or show mercy
- Sets up the mage as someone who must reckon with the responsibility of their power
- **Good path** → starts in Sky Harbor
- **Evil path** → starts in Winter Haven (they have seen what the warrior is heading toward before the warrior gets there)
- Mages with divine bloodline may have naturally stronger magic than peers — a quiet tension within the mage community

### The Thief
- Origin: a **chase sequence** — each leg involves a fight, escalating toward a final obstacle
- Moral choice at the end: kill the person blocking your escape (evil) or surrender (good)
- **Good path** → short jail sentence, released, begins the main game from a different position than other classes
- **Evil path** → on the run, hiding — possibly in a monster city or similar outcast location
- The jail ending creates a natural hook for how the party assembles later
- Thief class occupies a moral grey zone by design — motivations matter more than actions

### Party Assembly
- The player will eventually gain **two additional party members** (mage and thief)
- Party members share the player's alignment — encountered organically through story beats, not a hub recruitment screen
- Inspired by 8 Bit Adventure's approach to natural party formation
- Evil path trio and good path trio have fundamentally different group dynamics

### Divine Blood & Special Moves
- All three main characters are **divine children** — this is the unifying thread that draws them together
- Divine heritage grants access to **special moves beyond normal hominid capability**
- Special moves grow in complexity as the character levels up — inspired by Legend of Legaia and Final Fantasy 6
  - Early ranks: simple execution, accessible inputs
  - Higher ranks: more complex input combinations — the move feeling harder to execute mirrors the character pushing their power ceiling
  - A normal hominid literally cannot execute high-rank divine moves — their body and power ceiling won't allow it
- This gives divine blood a **gameplay expression** beyond lore flavor
- All three classes have their own special move trees reflecting their weapon and playstyle

## Skill Progression System (Planned)

### Move Tier Structure
- Ranks 1–4: Learning the move
- Rank 5: Move mastered — **Tier 1 complete**. Unlocks hidden combo potential with any other Rank 5 skill
- Rank 6: Move **tiers up** into Tier 2 — name changes, costs 1 less AP as a mastery reward
- Ranks 6–9: Damage scaling per rank, AP cost rises again as power increases
- Rank 9: **Tier 3 promotion** — name changes again, cost drops by 1 as another mastery reward
- Rank 10: Final form — maximum damage plus a **unique bonus effect** unique to each move
- The rhythm of cost relief at tier promotions (Rank 6 and Rank 9) rewards investment before the next tier ramps cost back up

### Skill Tree Reveal System
- Players only see **1–2 ranks ahead** of their current rank in the skill description
- Future ranks display as locked until the preceding rank is reached
- Rank 10 legendary effects are **never revealed in advance** — discovered on achievement
- Hidden combo moves (Rank 5+5 and Rank 10+10) are **completely invisible** until both skills meet the threshold — no hints, no ???, just sudden discovery
- This creates organic word-of-mouth and theory-crafting among players

### Example Progression — Power Strike
| Rank | Tier | Name | AP Cost | Damage | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1–4 | 1 | Power Strike | 3 | Scaling | Learning phase |
| 5 | 1 | Power Strike | 3 | 2x | Mastered — Rank 5 combo unlocked |
| 6 | 2 | Double Strike | 2 | 2x | Tier up — cost relief, same power |
| 7 | 2 | Double Strike | 3 | 2.5x | Power scaling resumes |
| 8 | 2 | Double Strike | 4 | 3x | |
| 9 | 3 | Triple Strike | 3 | 3.5x | Tier up — cost relief again |
| 10 | 3 | Quad Strike | 4 | 4x + **defence shred** | Legendary — Rank 10 combo unlocked |

- The defence shred at Rank 10 makes Quad Strike a **setup move**, not just a nuke — exposes the enemy for follow-up attacks
- Rewards chaining attacks and tactical sequencing rather than spamming the big hit
- No AP cost reduction at Rank 10 — the power is worth the price

### Skill Point Economy
- Skill points awarded at **2 per level**, with bonus milestone awards of **5 points** at levels 5, 10, 15, and 20 (instead of the usual 2) — approximately **52 points** available at Level 20 cap
- Ranking a single skill to 10 costs approximately 45 points (1+2+3...+9) — a near-total budget commitment
- Players must specialize hard — no single run can max everything, which is intentional
- **Rare upgrade items** exist as a pressure valve — found in hidden areas, boss drops, or tied to moral path choices — allowing additional investment beyond the base budget
- Skill investment is **optional at every rank** — unlocking a skill does not force point spending

### Hidden Combo Moves

#### Rank 5 + Rank 5 Combos (Warrior)
| Skill A | Skill B | Combo Move | Effect | AP Cost |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Power Strike R5 | War Cry R5 | **Power Charge** | Blended damage boost (1.5x) + moderate attack buff for 2 turns | 4 |
| First Aid R5 | War Cry R5 | **Rally Cry** | AoE heal for player + party | TBD |
| First Aid R5 | Power Strike R5 | **Power Heal** | Heal scales with damage output — hit hard, heal yourself | TBD |

- Rank 5+5 combos are **blended** — not as strong as using both skills at full power separately, but more AP efficient
- Designed as a "value right now" option when AP is tight, not a must-use replacement for the parent skills

#### Rank 10 + Rank 10 Combos (Warrior)
| Skill A | Skill B | Combo Move | Effect | AP Cost | Limit |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Quad Strike R10 | Warriors Cry R10 | **Warriors Scream** | Quad damage AOE + fear status on all enemies | TBD | Once per battle |

- Fear status reduces enemy damage output for X turns — debuffs the whole field
- Once per battle — powerful enough to be near game-breaking but weight of decision makes it legendary
- **Optimal sequence**: Quad Strike first (defence shred) → Warriors Scream (fear + AOE into exposed enemies)
- The naming ladder tells its own story of escalation: War Cry → Warriors Cry → Warriors Scream

## Weapon & Equipment Systems

### Weapon Core (Expanded Lore)
- A **relic of advanced pre-virus technology** — nano-tech that the current population perceives as magic
- On first acquisition it appears as a **strange living metal** that reacts to the player specifically
- The player invests **weapon points** to mold it to their playstyle — each weapon type has its own skill tree
- Better upgrades unlock as the Core is tiered up
- The Core reacts to the player because the player is a demigod — it was likely made for or by the civilization that created the hominid species
- **Slowly becoming sentient** is a possible late-game revelation — the bonding process over the full trilogy may not be purely mechanical
- The Weapon Core is essentially **alien technology to the Beast Gods** even though it originated on the same planet — two completely different technological philosophies (nano-tech vs biotech) developed in isolation

### Weapon Upgrade Questline
A four-tier upgrade path involving:
1. Standard upgrades available in Winter Haven
2. Sky Harbor gem enhancements
3. Dwarven city questline
4. Artificer NPC (Ignius) for top-tier crafting

### Legendary Armor Sets (Dual Path System)

Every class has **two versions of their ultimate armor set** — one tied to the good path, one to the evil path. Each set has **6 armor pieces** (weapon sits outside the set system as a separately upgraded item). Sets expand progressively as pieces are found, with bonuses scaling at each threshold.

**Good Path Set — Chimera Scale Armor**
- Organic nano-tech origin — feels like something earned from the natural world
- Base material: Chimera Scale (dropped by Young Chimera boss)
- Tone: natural, earned, alive

**Evil Path Set — Tainted Champion's Armor**
- Corrupted divine nano-tech — taken from something meant to protect, now warped
- Base material: Champion's Armor (dropped by Patronus)
- Tone: powerful, heavy, morally compromised
- The corruption is visible — the item description and flavor text reflect what you did to get it

**Shared Design Rules**
- Both sets use nano-tech that **morphs to fit the class** — a Warrior gets a shield piece, a Mage gets a focus piece, a Thief gets a bracer or cloak piece. Same item, different expression. Mirrors the Weapon Core philosophy.
- Sets **expand as additional pieces are found** — each piece builds on the last like a foundation being extended
- **Path locking with tease pieces**: 1–2 pieces from the opposite path are findable but orphaned without their set. Players see that the pieces interact but cannot complete the alternate set in a single playthrough. Designed to create curiosity and drive replays.
- Tease pieces carry flavor text like *"resonates with something distant"* or *"feels incomplete"* — implies depth without explaining it
- **Set bonus progression** (same structure for both paths):
  - 1 piece — minor bonus
  - 2 pieces — noticeable upgrade  
  - 4 pieces — passive effect unlocks

## The Roguelike Arena System
*(Post-Steam Early Access content — not in terminal demo or initial Steam launch)*

Inspired by the Ancient Cave in **Lufia II: Rise of the Sinistrals** — 99 floors, start with nothing, find gear as you descend, lose everything on death. Your version adds a crafting progression layer that carries between runs, making it more strategically deep while remaining punishing.

### How It Unlocks
- As the player clears arenas across the realm, each new arena is discovered with best gear intact
- On first clear it gets added to the roguelike pool
- The player finds the Prologue Arena early — but it grows in complexity as the main game world expands

### The Core Loop
1. Enter arena at **Level 1 with no gear** — only crafting components earned from previous runs
2. Fight through escalating floors, finding components and temporary gear
3. **Craft at break points** during the run using accumulated components
4. Clear the arena → sell craftables for good value, keep currency
5. **Lose → gear is gone**. Components and currency carry forward. Back to the start.

### Crafting Component System
- Components are earned across all arena runs and main game content
- Quality tiers: Poor → Normal → Uncommon → Rare → Legendary
- Combining upgrades quality: 2 Poor → 1 Normal → 3 Normal → 1 Uncommon → and so on up to Legendary
- Once a better version is created it becomes **purchasable with arena currency** at that tier going forward
- Example progression: Legendary Acid Sack + Legendary Brittle Skeleton Sword = dramatically improved run viability
- Selling completed craftables returns good value — players can cash out a successful run

### Arena Currency
- Earned every attempt regardless of completion — failed runs still feel productive
- **Arena-only resource** — stays entirely within the arena ecosystem, does not convert to main game gold
- Sits in the player's inventory as a tangible reminder of accumulated progress
- Emergency conversion to main game gold possible at extremely poor rate (desperation option only, not intended path)
- Thematic note: Beast Gods don't let fighters take arena power into the real world. What happens in the pit stays in the pit.

### Gear Rules
- All arena gear is **arena-only** — does not leave the dungeon
- Gear lost on death — creates real weight behind every crafting decision
- The crafting progression across runs is the true long term reward, not individual gear pieces

## Cosmology & Universe Lore (Expanded)

### The Planet
- Unnamed as of current development
- Located in **another solar system** — not Earth, not the solar system
- The Milky Way has been significantly explored and colonized by advanced humans
- 100 million years have passed since the original colonization event
- Countless cycles of civilizational rise and collapse have occurred — advanced technology is no longer fully understood by the current population

### The Hominid Species (Player's Species)
- Not modern humans — **hominids**, a species genetically engineered from primates by advanced humans
- Created deliberately: advanced humans, having conquered the galaxy, turned to **creating life** as the next frontier
- The parallel to real-world ancient astronaut mythology is intentional — it resonates because the idea recurs across human cultures
- Advanced humans feed on **emotional energy** — worship, admiration, devotion — and the hominid species was partly created to provide this
- The relationship was likely genuinely warm, not purely exploitative — the creators probably cared about their creation
- Multiple hominid-derived species exist across different planets, shaped by different environments over millions of years

### The Virus
- A **naturally evolved pathogen** discovered on another planet — not engineered, not a weapon
- Spread faster than quarantine could contain — entire planets of advanced humans wiped out before the mechanism was understood
- Animals were **immune** — only humans/hominids were affected
- The speed of spread made planetary quarantine impossible; attempting it only prolonged the collapse
- After 100 million years, advanced humans now understand how to prevent its spread
- There is no villain behind the virus — it is pure tragedy, the universe being indifferent
- Everything that followed — Beast God civilization, Winter Haven, the player character's existence — traces back to this single natural event

### Advanced Humans (The Creators)
- Still exist — **quintillions of near-immortal beings** across the galaxy
- Do not die of old age — can be killed by sufficiently advanced beings but are otherwise indefinitely lived
- Most exist in **dormancy/stasis** — the food supply required to sustain quintillions of immortals across 100 million years demands it
- Feed on emotional energy through **worship, admiration, and devotion** — not death and suffering (though evil factions may use darker methods)
- Govern themselves through a **fiefdom-style hierarchy**: peasants → farm hands → warrior/merchant class → nobility → kings → higher kings (multiple layers)
- One ultimate ruler may exist at the top — deliberately left undefined for now
- Small numbers actively guard certain locations — Sky Harbor tower is guarded by advanced humans
- The Beast Gods and advanced humans exist in a **cold war** — mutual non-interference maintained by the threat of intergalactic war

### The Beast Gods
- Evolved from animals brought to the planet by the original human colonists
- 100 million years of evolution after the virus eliminated the humans produced sentient apex species
- Focused on **biotech rather than advanced mechanical technology** — a completely different developmental path from humans
- Not universally evil — their relationship with hominids mirrors the diversity of human-animal relationships today
  - Species that coexisted peacefully with humans evolved toward coexistence
  - Species that were prey/predator relationships evolved toward conflict and exploitation
- The Winter Haven farm is not unique cruelty — it is one end of a spectrum that advanced humans also occupy, just at the darker extreme
- Exist in vast numbers, many in dormancy — parallel structure to advanced humans
- **Cold war with advanced humans**: neither side interferes significantly in the other's affairs; too much interference risks intergalactic war

### The Cold War Balance
- Both advanced humans and Beast Gods harvest emotional energy — different methods, same fundamental need
- Neither side wants open conflict — the cost would be incomprehensible at galactic scale
- The planet exists in an uneasy equilibrium: Beast Gods control Winter Haven, advanced humans control Sky Harbor tower
- The player character — a demigod wildcard — is **not bound by the cold war agreement**
- As the player's power and reputation grow, both sides will begin to take notice
- A fully realized demigod is not peer-level to either side but is unpredictable enough to matter

### The Player Character's Divine Heritage
- Child of a **Divine mother** (one of Sky Harbor's guardians) and a father with divine blood
- The mother cannot openly raise the child without risking discovery — watches from Sky Harbor
- The father has enemies regardless of the path he took — cannot safely raise a child while pursued
- The player is **not the first demigod** — parallels to Greek gods having children with mortals are intentional
  - Powerful enough beings make their own rules
  - The hierarchy tolerates it because enforcement is impractical
  - Other demigods exist at various stages of awareness about what they are
- **Diluted divine blood** in ordinary hominids can surface as enhanced magical ability or brief moments of power — the Warrior's moral choice moment is one example
- The player's power is masked in early game: Winter Haven's dungeon masks their power signature; Sky Harbor's protections shield the child
- As story choices ripple outward, awareness of the player's true nature will grow on both sides
- The player begins as a nobody beneath cosmic notice — by Game 3 they are a genuine variable in the galactic cold war

### Roguelike Arena — Expanded Design (April 2026)

**Core Loop**
Every run starts at Level 1 regardless of main game progression. There are no permanent stat carryovers between runs — skill expression and build decisions are the equalizer. A veteran player with a full meta pool will outperform a new player, but only because they know how to spend it, not because their stats are higher. Runs take approximately 30 minutes for the base arena. Additional arenas unlock as the player explores the main game world, extending potential run length and score ceiling as content expands.

**Persistent Meta Pool**
Gold earned in arena runs accumulates in a persistent pool that exists independently of the main game economy. The pool never resets. Every run contributes to it regardless of score or outcome — even a bad run is never wasted. The pool is spent at an arena-exclusive merchant between runs to purchase gear, accessories, and consumables for the next attempt. This creates a natural on-ramp: early runs are learning runs that also fund future attempts. The pool is also a rapid build experimentation tool — want to test a full DEF tank build? Buy the heaviest armour in the pool and run it. Want to test bleed/DoT stacking? Load up on accessories. No commitment, no consequence beyond the run itself.

**Score System**
Each run generates a score based on:
- Fights won and fights lost
- Total turns taken (efficiency bonus for fast clears)
- Damage dealt, damage blocked, damage taken
- Breakdown by attack type (basic / specials / DoT)
- Tier bonus: clearing each arena tier under a turn threshold applies a score multiplier
- Completion bonus: clearing all currently unlocked arenas in a single run

The score ceiling grows as the player unlocks more arenas through main game exploration. A player who has only cleared the Winter Haven arena has a lower maximum possible score than one who has unlocked Sky Harbor and beyond. This ties roguelike progression directly to main game investment without gating it behind story completion.

**Arena Unlocks**
Arenas are unlocked by exploring and progressing in the main game — not by spending pool gold. Each major location in the world has its own arena with its own enemy roster, mechanics, and tier structure. The further the player explores, the more complex and rewarding the roguelike becomes. Clearing deeper arenas faster awards higher tier bonuses and contributes more to the meta pool per run.

**Build Identity**
The combination of point-scarcity at Level 1, pool-funded gear choices, and the 30-minute run window creates conditions for emergent build identities without a class select screen:
- Full aggression: dump pool into ATK gear, max Power Strike, accept low DEF
- Tank: heavy armour from pool, invest points into Defence Break and First Aid, survive longer
- DoT/bleed: accessory stack from pool, let damage over time do the work
- Hybrid: split pool across ATK and DEF gear, broader skill investment, lower ceiling but more consistent

No two runs look the same if the player is experimenting. Veteran players will develop preferred opening builds and refine them over many runs, which is exactly the retention loop the mode is designed to create.

**Post-Game Role**
After completing the main story, the roguelike arena becomes the primary endgame sandbox. All arenas are accessible, the full meta pool is available, and there are no story stakes — just optimization and score chasing. This is where players test the limits of the build system, chase leaderboard scores, and replay content they enjoyed from the main game in a consequence-free environment. The mode is designed to keep the game alive long after the story is finished without requiring new story content to sustain it.

## Death Defier — Skill Design (Warrior, Level 5 Unlock)

Death Defier is the Warrior's capstone skill — the culmination of a fighter who has stared at death long enough to push back against it on purpose. It is not a heal. It is a declaration.

### Mechanic
The player spends their turn to activate Death Defier. It sets a passive flag. If the player's HP would drop to 0 before their next turn, they survive instead with HP equal to the rank threshold. The flag is consumed — one use per fight.

| Rank | Survival HP | AP Cost | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 1 HP | 3 AP | Barely alive — one hit ends it |
| 2 | 10% max HP | 3 AP | Small buffer |
| 3 | 20% max HP | 4 AP | Meaningful breathing room |
| 4 | 30% max HP | 4 AP | Real second chance |
| 5 | 40% max HP | 5 AP | Maximum survivability |
| 6 (Tier 2) | 40% max HP | 4 AP | Cost relief at tier up |

### Flavour
- River Spirit (free version from lore event): equivalent to rank 1-2 effect, always 0 AP
- The River Spirit version is a gift. The skill version is earned.
- AP cost scales with rank: 3/3/4/4/5 — reflects growing power investment. Tier 2 (Undying, rank 6) drops back to 4 AP as the standard mastery cost relief.

### Design Notes
- One use per fight — not spammable, high decision weight
- Works against DoT deaths too (poison/burn/acid kills)
- Does not proc twice in the same fight (death_defier_used flag)
- The rank 5 "free" version makes it a serious build option for a dedicated HP-tank warrior
